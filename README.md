
**1. Define the roles in Scrum?**

 - Product Owner: The product owner is an individual who is responsible
   for increasing the ROI by determining product features, prioritizing
   these features into a list, what needs to be focused on the upcoming
   sprint, and much more. These are constantly re-prioritized and
   refined. 
 - Scrum Master: This individual helps the team in learning to    apply
   Scrum to ensure optimum business value. The scrum master    removes
   impediments, shields the team from distractions, and enables    them
   to adopt agile practices.     Scrum Team: They are a collection of   
   individuals who work together to ensure that the requirements of the 
   stakeholders are delivered.
 - The Developers: The Developers are the people in the Scrum Team who are committed to creating any aspect  of a usable Increment each Sprint. The term "Developer" doesn't just mean programmers; it includes anyone contributing to the actual delivery. They focus on the "How." 

**2. What are the responsibilities of the Scrum Team?**

The Scrum Team is one that’s self-organizing and involves five to seven members. The following are their responsibilities:

 - Working products must be developed and delivered during each sprint.
   Ownership and transparency must be ensured for the work assigned to
   the team members. 
   Correct and crisp information must be provided to
   ensure a successful daily scrum meeting. 
   They must collaborate with
   the team and themselves.

Collective Accountability:
- Project Tracking, the whole team updates and tracks the Sprint Backlog.
- Quality Control, the whole team owns quality from day one (built-in testing).
- Problem Solving, team surfaces blocks early; Scrum Master helps facilitate resolution.
- Success/Failure, the team wins together or fails together.

**3. What are the Artifacts of the Scrum Process?**

 - Product Backlog: It is a list that consists of new features, changes 
   to features, bug fixes, changes to the infrastructure, and other
   activities to ensure a particular output can be obtained. This is owned and managed by the PO, and its commitment is the 
Product Goal.
 - Sprint Backlog: It is a subset of the product backlog that contains
   tasks focused on by the team to satisfy the sprint goal. Teams
   first identify the tasks to be completed from the product backlog.
   These are then added to the sprint backlog. This is owned by the Developers and its commitment is the Sprint Goal.
  - Product Increment: It is a combination of all product backlog items
   completed in a sprint and the value of previous sprints' increments.
   The output must be in  usable condition, even if the product
   owner doesn’t release it. This is owned by the entire Scrum Team and its commitment is the Definition of Done.

**4. What happens in Daily Stand-up sessions?**

Stand-up sessions are daily discussions that take place and are usually 15 minutes long. Daily Stand-up sessions help understand:

 - What tasks went well
 - What tasks were completed
 - What tasks are pending, and
 - The obstacles the team is facing
 - The meeting helps in understanding the overall scope and status of
   the project. Further discussions can take place after the stand-up
   sessions

This is an opportunity for the Scrum Master to help the team remove the possible roadbloacks and have quick feedback.

**5. What is Sprint 0 and Spike?**

In professional Scrum environments, "Sprint 0" and "Spike" are terms used frequently during backlog refinement and architectural planning, though only one of them (Spike) is actually recognized as an official agile practice.

Sprint 0 refers to the small amount of effort put in to create a rough skeleton of the product backlog. It also includes insights towards estimating the release of products. Sprint 0 is required for: 

 - Creating the project skeleton, along with research spikes
 - Keeping minimal design
 - Developing some stories completely
 - Having low velocity and being lightweight
 - The spike is a set of activities that involve Extreme Programming
   (XP) for research, design, investigation, creating POCs, etc.
 - The spike aims to reduce risks of the technical approach, helping
   gain knowledge to better understand requirements and improve
   reliability. 

**6. What is a User Story?**

A user story is an agile software development/ project management tool that provides teams with simple, natural language explanations of one or more features of the project that’s written from the perspective of the end-user.
The user story doesn’t go into detail but only mentions how certain types of work will bring value to the end-user. The end-user, in this case, could be an external component or an internal customer/colleague within the organization.
They also form the building block of agile frameworks like epics and other initiatives.
They ensure that the teams work towards the goals of the organization, with the help of epics and initiatives.
The requirements to make a user story a reality are added later, after discussions with the team.
They are recorded on post-it notes, index cards, or project management software.

In general the User Stories are the base of the project, because they integrate the initial information shared by the stakeholders and integrates a description everyone can agree to work with and understands. 

**7. How are user stories, epics, and tasks different?**

 - User Stories: They provide the team with simple explanations of the
   business’ requirements created from the end user's perspective.
 - Epics: An epic is a collection of related user stories. They are
   usually large and complex.
 - Tasks: Tasks are used to break down user stories further. They’re the
   smallest unit in Scrum that is used to track work. A person or a team
   of two people usually work on a task.

Basically, Epics are groups of user stories that display a narrative, but in digestible smaller pieces.
The tasks, are compact work units for easy track and assignment.

**8. How is Estimation Done in a Scrum Project?**

The estimation of user stories is done based on their difficulty 
A particular scale is used to assess the difficulty of the user stories. Some type of scales are: 

 - Numeric Sizing (1 - 10)
 - T-shirt Sizes (S, M, L, XL…)
 - Fibonacci Series (1, 2, 3, 5, 8…)
 - Dog breeds (Great Dane, Chihuahua…)

I like the Fibonacci Series because it's really easy to understand and associate quickly, and as numbers, you can make estatistics. One regular practice is to read the user story and ask the team to assign a number according to the difficulty they think it represents, after that, we can discuss those numbers and present the reasons to keep/change the current level, so, as a group, we get to a group understanding and alignment.

**9. What are some risks in Scrum? How are they handled?**

Some types of risks in Scrum are:

 - Budget: The risk of exceeding budgets. You have to manage the expectatives of the stakeholders and also let them think about new features that can be part of a new version of the project involving a new project, not everythina can/should be part of the current iterarion.
 - People (team): Team members need to be of appropriate skill and capability Sprint. You have to check with them if they need some time to update their knowledge or practice their skills about this project, so we can create a space for that, so in the end, we get to the goal.
 - Duration and deliverables: Exceeding the duration, addition of  the scope of work. Again, you have to manage the expectatives of the stakeholders and also let them think about new features that can be part of a new version of the project involving a new project, not everythina can/should be part of the current iterarion.
 - Product (user stories, epics): Having ill-defined user stories and epics. From the begining I work with the PO about the methods and how to execute them in order to minimize this risk, so they're better suited for the task.
 - Knowledge and capability: Having the appropriate resources. About the capability, you must take a look on the history of projects like the one running currently, and based on that info, make the capabilities projection. 

**9.1. How are they handled?** 
Managing risks involves identifying, assessing, analyzing, defining, and implementing risk responses, monitoring, and managing them. These are done on a continual basis right from the starting of the project until completion. Requirement: `I want to have notifications in the App when there is new content added` 

      The User Story

Story title: Screen popup notifications

User story Narrative: As a registered app user,
I want to receive an OS-level push notification of new content added while the app is closed,,
So that I can instantly jump directly to the new content without manually checking the app.

Business Value: Maximizes daily active users (DAU), improves user retention, and drives immediate engagement upon content release.

      Technical & Design Notes (Context for the Team)

- Security Constraint: Push notifications should only be sent to devices with an active, authenticated user session token. If a user explicitly logs out, the push token must be unregistered from the backend.
- Deep Linking: The notification payload must include a deep link URL pointing directly to the specific content ID.
- UI/UX: A new popup message window must be displayed notifyng about the new content available in the app. 
- Limitation: To prevent spamming the user's lock screen, use an OS notification group/collapse key. If a 4th piece of content is added before the user opens the app, the oldest notification in the system tray is replaced, capping the stack at 3.

      Acceptance Criteria (Given-When-Then)

- Scenario 1: App is in background/Device locked (Logged In)
      * Given a user is logged into the app, but the app is currently closed or the device is locked,
      * When new content is published by the system,
      * Then the device OS should display a push notification template: "New Content Available: [Content Title]".

- Scenario 2: Deep linking to content
      * Given a user has received a new content push notification on their lock screen,
      * When they tap the notification,
      * Then the app should open and bypass the home screen, deep-linking the user directly to the new content detail view.

- Scenario 3: The Logged-Out State (Security Protection)
      * Given a user has explicitly logged out of their account on the device,
      * When new content is published by the system,
      * Then the system must not send a push notification to that device.

- Scenario 4: Handling Multiple Unread Notifications (The 3-Cap Rule)
      * Given the user has 3 unread content notifications sitting in their device system tray,
      * When a 5th piece of content is published,
      * Then the OS notification engine should overwrite/remove the oldest unread notification, maintaining a maximum of 3 notifications from the app in the tray.
